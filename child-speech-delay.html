IS THIS TYPICAL?

A rough guide to speech & language milestones

Every child develops at their own pace, but these general ranges are a useful reference point. If your child is noticeably behind on more than one of these, an assessment is worth doing.

12–24 months

First words emerging, understanding simple instructions, pointing to communicate, and combining two words together by around 24 months ("more milk", "daddy go").

2–3 years

Short sentences of 3-4 words, vocabulary growing quickly, asking simple questions, and being mostly understood by familiar adults, even if not every sound is clear yet.

3–4 years

Longer sentences, telling simple stories, being understood by unfamiliar listeners most of the time, and asking "why" and "how" questions.

When to get checked sooner

No words by 18 months, loss of skills your child previously had, very limited eye contact or response to their name, or persistent frustration when trying to communicate.

WHAT TO EXPECT

What actually happens, step by step

Most parents are more anxious about the first visit than their child is. Here's what it actually involves.

01 — INTAKE

History & concerns

A conversation about your child's development, your specific concerns, and any relevant medical or hearing history.

02 — OBSERVATION

Play-based assessment

The therapist observes and gently engages your child through play, which is far less stressful for a child than formal testing alone.

03 — RESULTS

Plain-language explanation

You'll be told clearly where your child stands, what's typical, and what (if anything) needs attention — no jargon, no alarm.

04 — PLAN

Therapy plan

If therapy is recommended, a session frequency and home-practice plan is built around your child's specific needs and your routine.
